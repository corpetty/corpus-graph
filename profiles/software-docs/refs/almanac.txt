Web Almanac — caching and rendering notes (illustrative source text).

Across the pages measured in the annual crawl, the large majority ship markup
that is substantially static: most documents do not require per-request server
rendering to produce their primary content. Sites that do render on the server
tend to pair it with aggressive caching to keep per-request cost bounded.

Cache hit ratios are sensitive to how URLs change between deploys. When asset
URLs churn on every release, caches see a flood of cold keys and hit ratios fall
until the new keys warm up. Teams that hash content into the URL (so a given byte
sequence always maps to the same key) see steadier hit ratios, at the cost of
needing an explicit invalidation or tagging mechanism when content is superseded
rather than overwriting a key in place.

The report also notes that time-to-first-byte is dominated by origin work on
server-rendered routes, which is why streaming and edge caching show up
repeatedly as mitigations. The headline takeaway for framework defaults is that
server rendering buys freshness and a simpler mental model but must be paired
with a caching strategy, while static delivery is cheaper to serve but staler and
shifts the freshness burden onto the build and deploy pipeline.
